Spatial Analysis of Environmental Influences on Adolescent Mental Health in Freetown, Sierra Leone

Alethea Desrosiers, PhD & Cara M. Antonaccio, PhD, MSPH

Brown University, Warren Alpert Medical School, Department of Psychiatry & Human Behavior

Introduction & Background

  • In post-conflict urban settings like Freetown, Sierra Leone, environmental factors influencing adolescent mental health are not well understood, especially their geographic patterns.
  • Understanding the spatial distribution of mental health challenges can help target interventions and plan resource allocation more effectively.
  • Objective: This study aimed to identify geographic hotspots of elevated mental health symptoms among adolescents in Freetown and to analyze the associations between these symptoms and various contextual risk factors.

Methods

  • Study Design: A spatial epidemiological analysis was conducted.
  • Data Source: Baseline data from a study on a youth-focused mental health intervention.
  • Participants: 1,200 adolescents in Freetown, Sierra Leone.
  • Measures:
    • Mental Health: Hopkins Symptom Checklist (HSCL).
    • Contextual Factors: Socioeconomic Status & Proximity to Environmental Hazards.
  • Analysis: Thematic cartography and spatial regression models were used to examine associations between mental health symptoms and key contextual factors.

Results

  • Analyses revealed spatial heterogeneity (i.e., "hotspots") of mental health symptoms among adolescents based on school location.
  • Adolescent mental health was associated with school proximity to key environmental risk factors (i.e., historical landslide locations and the Freetown waste disposal site).

Conclusions & Implications

  • This study provides key insights into the geospatial dynamics of adolescent mental health in a resource-constrained, post-conflict urban setting.
  • The identification of geographic hotspots suggests opportunities for targeted interventions and resource allocation.
  • The link between mental health and environmental factors may indicate a need to address these determinants to improve adolescent well-being.
  • Integrating spatial analysis into research on adolescent mental health is a valuable tool for understanding risk and resilience.
